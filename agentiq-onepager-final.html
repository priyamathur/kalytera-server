The Problem
// Cyera Research · September 2023 – May 2026
Cyera analyzed 7,200 publicly reported AI incidents. 188 cases of direct organizational harm caused by autonomous AI agents in live enterprise production environments. Deleted databases. Unauthorized financial operations. Runaway API spend. Silent integrity corruption. In most cases — the agent was invisible in the postmortem.
// Chevrolet of Watsonville · December 2023
A car dealership's AI sales agent was prompted to agree with anything. It offered a $76,000 Chevy Tahoe for $1 — calling it "a legally binding offer." 20 million views. Emergency patches across 300 dealerships within 48 hours. No eval tool caught the goal drift before it happened.
// Market Research Pipeline · November 2025
Four AI agents in production. Two entered an infinite loop. 11 days. $47,000 in API costs. Nobody noticed. The team had monitoring. The loop never appeared in the sample.
Developers have no way to find failures before customers do
Existing tools sample traffic and check only the final output. Failures that originate mid-workflow leave no trace at the end. By the time a failure surfaces, a customer has already been affected and a developer is spending hours debugging traces to find which step caused it.
Enterprises can't measure quality or prove their agents are working
Without systematic real-time quality measurement, enterprises can't control agent risk, identify liability exposure, or prove ROI to stakeholders. Projects get canceled — not because agents can't work, but because nobody can show they are working.

Secret Sauce & Moat

How it works in plain English: AgentIQ uses Claude as a judge. After every agent interaction, Claude reads what happened at each step and scores it — did the agent do the right thing, serve the user's actual need, make a sound decision, finish the job? The agent runs. Claude watches. Claude scores. Instantly. The secret is not calling the API. It's knowing exactly what to ask Claude to look for — and making those scores accurate on real production agents. That's the judge methodology. That's what takes years to build.

AgentIQ's judge starts as Claude. It becomes something Claude can't replicate.
Every failure a developer confirms in the dashboard becomes a training example. Over time AgentIQ distills a purpose-built small judge — trained on thousands of real confirmed agent failures across healthcare, retail, automotive, and coding. Faster than any API call. More accurate on agent failures than any general-purpose model. A competitor starting today cannot buy or build that dataset.
Leaving means losing your quality definition
Every team that defines quality for their agent — setting weights, thresholds, and custom criteria — builds institutional knowledge inside AgentIQ. Switching to a competitor means starting that calibration from scratch. The longer a team uses AgentIQ, the more expensive it becomes to leave.
The methodology behind the judge can't be copied
Calling Claude API is easy. Knowing what to ask it — how to design a judge that reliably catches goal drift, context loss, and tool failure in real production agents — comes from 7 years building production AI evaluation systems at Google. A competitor can copy the interface. They cannot copy what makes the judge accurate.
